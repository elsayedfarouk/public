Text: I know this take will get hate, but I’ve been wanting to address some unpopular topics for a while, and this one has been on my mind: being a creator can be harder than a 9-5 job. It all depends on what we mean by “harder.”

First off, I dislike the term “9-5 job.” It’s so vague - are we talking about a brain surgeon, a McDonald’s worker, or an electrician? The term doesn’t capture what the job actually involves. And there’s variance between content creators too, depending on what type of content they make. Some people’s content is definitely harder to make, so mass grouping them doesn’t really make sense either. But if we define “harder” as requiring a unique talent or skill, then being most types of creators is undoubtedly more specialized than most traditional jobs. The reality is, 99.9% of content creators fail. In a world with short attention spans, keeping people’s interest is no small feat, and earning minimum wage as a content creator is certainly more challenging than doing so in a typical minimum-wage job. In most jobs, you clock in, do the work, and get paid. As a creator, you can put in countless hours without seeing any return, making it far more difficult to reach even that basic level of income.

Now, some of you may argue, well, it’s not harder than professions like a doctor, lawyer, engineer, or construction worker, and you have a point. But I’m not sure many in those roles could succeed as creators either. The skills are different.

However, when people say a creator’s job isn’t “hard,” they usually mean one of three things:

1.“It’s easier because creators make more money.” This is a myth. Assuming all creators are like Adin Ross or Kai Cenat is like assuming every basketball player is LeBron James. The truth is, 99% of content creators don’t even make minimum wage—most don’t earn anything at all. The 0.01% that do make it often struggle for years, working for free before they see any success. For every millionaire YouTuber, there are thousands who can’t monetize their channels. It’s not about just turning on a camera and getting paid. It’s countless hours of work, often with no guarantee of return.

2.“It’s not useful or important.” Some content might not contribute much to the world, and there’s validity to that point. But if we’re talking about the amount of skill required and difficulty, being a streamer or content creator is unarguably “hard.” The level of creativity, technical knowledge, and adaptability needed to succeed is immense. Important doesn’t necessarily equal hard. For example, a crossing guard has an important job, but it’s not particularly difficult. Just because some content might not seem to add value doesn’t mean creating it is easy.

3.“It’s easy because it doesn’t take much physical labor.” While it’s true that content creation isn’t physically demanding like construction or other manual labor jobs, that doesn’t mean it’s easy. We wouldn’t call Einstein’s job as a physicist or a judge’s job easy just because they weren’t out in the sun or using physical labor. Those jobs are hard too, but in different ways. I reject the idea that just because you’re not outside or working in a chair, what you do is easy - honestly, I think that’s wrong. Not to say labor jobs aren’t hard, because they definitely are, but yeah. The mental and creative demands of being a content creator are intense. You have to constantly generate new ideas, stay relevant, and engage with an audience that can make you fall off any second. The stress of having to consistently perform, often with little to no immediate reward, takes a toll. Physical labor is just one aspect of what makes a job hard, mental strain, emotional investment, and the pressure to innovate are equally challenging.

In reality, I think the whole argument is unproductive and, frankly, stupid. Stupid because you can’t rank jobs. Everyone is needed in a way and adds some sort of value. We shouldn’t be comparing how hard or easy our struggle is.
